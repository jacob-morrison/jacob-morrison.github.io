Hi! I work on making language models more capable and more open — and on understanding their environmental and societal impacts. I'm a Ph.D. student at the University of Washington, where I'm advised by Noah Smith and supported by an NSF Computer Science Graduate Fellowship, and a student researcher on the Open Ecosystem team at Ai2, where I help build the Olmo family of fully open language models.

Before my Ph.D., I worked on code generation at Google [x] and platform health at Twitter, and in a previous life I was a software engineer at Tableau and Google. See my CV for the full story.

Research

My research focuses on making language models broadly useful and reliable. Recently, that has meant data-centric methods for improving model capabilities — including the Tülu post-training recipes and the RewardBench evaluations for reward models — and measuring the environmental footprint of developing language models, from hardware manufacturing through deployment. Underneath all of it is a commitment to open science: I'm a core contributor to Ai2's Olmo project, which releases strong language models together with the training data, code, and infrastructure behind them.

Policy

I also work directly with policymakers to help ground AI policy in how the technology actually works. I've served as a public policy lead at Ai2 and as a member of the City of Seattle's Generative AI Policy Advisory Group, and I've written on the legal and regulatory questions raised by generative AI — from legal risk and liability to the First Amendment — which you can find on my publications page.
